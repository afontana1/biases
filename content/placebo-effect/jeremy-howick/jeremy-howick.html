Jeremy Howick is a Canadian-born, British residing clinical epidemiologist and philosopher of science. He is known for his research on evidence-based medicine and the philosophy of medicine, including the use of placebos in clinical practice and clinical trials.[1][2] He is the author of over 100 peer-reviewed papers, as well as two books, The Philosophy of Evidence-Based Medicine in 2011,[3] and Doctor You in 2017.[4] In 2016, he received the Dawkins & Strutt grant from the British Medical Association to study pain treatment.[5] He publishes in Philosophy of Medicine and medical journals.[6]

Early life and education[edit]

Howick, a native of Montreal, Canada, is a graduate of Westmount High School. He holds a Bachelor of Arts in Engineering from the Dartmouth College, and graduate degrees from The London School of Economics and the University of Oxford. His PhD in Philosophy of medicine at the London School of Economics was conducted under the supervision of Professors Nancy Cartwright and John Worrall, with a thesis entitled Philosophical essentials in evidence-based medicine: Evaluating the epistemological role of double blinding and placebo controls, published in 2008.[7] He is the Director of the Oxford Empathy Programme[8] at the Faculty of Philosophy, University of Oxford. His paternal grandfather Wilfrid Howick is a Member of the Order of Canada for his philanthropic work.[9]

International Rowing[edit]

As a freshman at Dartmouth College, Jeremy learned to row. He subsequently competed international's for Canada at the 1994 World Championships, and won a silver medal at the 1994 Commonwealth Games.[10] He also competed in The Boat Race 1996 representing Oxford.

Professional background[edit]

Howick is known for his research which combines Philosophy of medicine with medical research (especially Evidence-based medicine, which he mostly defends,.[11] He has done extensive research on placebo effects and empathy, focusing on how these can help treat pain.[12][13] Together with Muir Gray, he founded the Oxford Empathy Programme,[14] and the Oxford Philosophy and Medicine Network.[15] His research has been translated for popular audiences in his book Doctor You.[16]

Broadcasting[edit]

Howick designed a trial of placebo treatments for back pain for a BBC Horizon documentary.[17]
